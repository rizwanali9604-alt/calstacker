How to Save Maximum Tax as a Salaried Employee India 2026

Legal tax-saving strategies under Section 80C, 80D, HRA, NPS and more for FY 2026-27

Advertisement

Every salaried employee in India faces the same annual question: how do I legally reduce the tax deducted from my salary? With two tax regimes, dozens of deduction sections, and changing rebate limits in Budget 2025, tax planning in 2026 requires more attention than ever. The good news is that the Income Tax Act provides well-defined, legitimate pathways to lower your tax liability — from Provident Fund contributions that happen automatically to strategic use of HRA, NPS, and health insurance deductions.

This guide covers every major tax-saving option available to salaried employees in FY 2026-27. You will understand how Section 80C, 80D, and 80CCD(1B) work with real numbers, learn HRA exemption rules for metro and non-metro cities, and see a side-by-side comparison of old vs new tax regime for an ₹8 lakh salary. We also walk through a cautionary real scenario where a ₹6 lakh earner paid ₹18,000 in extra tax by choosing the wrong regime, plus four common mistakes that cost salaried professionals thousands every year. Use this alongside our income tax calculator to find your optimal strategy.

Section 80C — Save Up to ₹1.5 Lakh

Section 80C is the most widely used tax-saving provision in India, allowing a deduction of up to ₹1,50,000 from your taxable income under the old tax regime. For someone in the 20% tax bracket, fully utilising 80C saves ₹31,200 in tax (including 4% cess). In the 30% bracket, the saving rises to ₹46,800. The following investments and expenses qualify:

Employee Provident Fund (EPF): Your monthly PF deduction (up to ₹1,800/month or ₹21,600/year) counts toward 80C automatically. Most employees already have ₹21,600 accounted for without any extra effort. Public Provident Fund (PPF): A 15-year government-backed scheme currently offering attractive interest rates, with a minimum ₹500 and maximum ₹1,50,000 annual contribution. ELSS Mutual Funds: Equity-linked savings schemes with a three-year lock-in — the only 80C option with equity market exposure and wealth creation potential. Life Insurance Premiums: Premiums paid for self, spouse, and children qualify, but the deduction is capped relative to the sum assured under Section 80C(3). NSC, Tax-Saving FDs, SSY: Fixed-income options with lock-in periods ranging from five to twenty-one years. Home Loan Principal: The principal component of your home loan EMI qualifies under 80C (interest qualifies separately under Section 24b). Tuition Fees: School or college fees for up to two children.

Plan your 80C allocation early in the financial year rather than rushing into tax-saving FDs or insurance policies in February. A balanced approach — EPF plus ELSS plus PPF — builds wealth while maximising the deduction.

Section 80D — Health Insurance Deduction

Section 80D provides deductions for health insurance premiums, encouraging families to maintain medical coverage. Under the old regime, you can claim up to ₹25,000 for premiums paid for yourself, spouse, and dependent children. An additional ₹25,000 is available for parents under 60, or ₹50,000 if parents are senior citizens (60+). If both you and your parents are senior citizens, the combined maximum reaches ₹1,00,000. Preventive health check-up expenses up to ₹5,000 are included within these limits (not over and above).

Even if your employer provides group health insurance, purchasing a personal top-up or family floater policy is worth it for both coverage and tax savings. A ₹15,000 annual premium saves ₹4,680 in tax at the 30% bracket. Under the new tax regime, Section 80D deductions are not available — another factor favouring the old regime for employees with family health insurance costs.

Section 80CCD(1B) — Additional NPS Deduction of ₹50,000

The National Pension System (NPS) offers a unique tax advantage: an additional deduction of up to ₹50,000 under Section 80CCD(1B), independent of the ₹1.5 lakh 80C limit. This means a salaried employee can effectively deduct up to ₹2,00,000 combined through 80C and 80CCD(1B). NPS contributions can be made through employer payroll deduction or directly via the eNPS portal. At the 30% tax bracket, the full ₹50,000 NPS contribution saves approximately ₹15,600 annually.

NPS is a retirement-focused product with partial equity exposure (up to 75% in active choice for younger subscribers). Withdrawals at retirement are partially tax-exempt — 60% of the corpus can be withdrawn tax-free as lump sum, with 40% mandatorily used to purchase an annuity. The lock-in until age 60 makes NPS best suited for long-term retirement planning rather than short-term tax saving. If you are already maxing out 80C through EPF and ELSS, NPS is the most powerful additional deduction available under the old regime.

HRA Exemption Rules

House Rent Allowance exemption is one of the most valuable tax benefits for salaried employees living on rent, but it is available only under the old tax regime. The exempt amount is the minimum of three calculations: (1) actual HRA received from your employer, (2) 50% of basic salary for metro cities (Mumbai, Delhi, Kolkata, Chennai, Bangalore, Hyderabad) or 40% for non-metro cities, and (3) actual annual rent paid minus 10% of basic salary.

Example: Basic salary ₹30,000/month (₹3,60,000/year), HRA received ₹15,000/month (₹1,80,000/year), annual rent ₹1,44,000 (₹12,000/month) in Bangalore. Calculation 1: ₹1,80,000. Calculation 2: 50% × ₹3,60,000 = ₹1,80,000. Calculation 3: ₹1,44,000 − ₹36,000 = ₹1,08,000. Exempt HRA = ₹1,08,000 (the minimum). You must submit rent receipts and your landlord's PAN if annual rent exceeds ₹1 lakh. If you live in your own house or with parents without paying rent, HRA is fully taxable — though paying rent to parents (with proper documentation) is legally permissible if they declare the rental income.

Old vs New Tax Regime: ₹8 LPA Example

Consider Rahul, earning ₹8,00,000 gross annual salary with ₹3,20,000 basic (40%), paying ₹1,20,000 annual rent in Pune (non-metro), with EPF of ₹21,600, ELSS of ₹50,000, health insurance premium of ₹18,000, and NPS contribution of ₹50,000 under 80CCD(1B).

Old Regime Calculation: Gross income ₹8,00,000. Less standard deduction ₹50,000 = ₹7,50,000. Less 80C (EPF ₹21,600 + ELSS ₹50,000 = ₹71,600, capped usage ₹71,600). Less 80D ₹18,000. Less 80CCD(1B) ₹50,000. Less HRA exemption approximately ₹84,000 (rent ₹1,20,000 minus 10% of basic ₹32,000 = ₹88,000, capped against HRA received). Taxable income ≈ ₹5,26,400. Tax per old regime slabs ≈ ₹13,000–15,000 plus cess. Estimated total tax: approximately ₹14,000–16,000.

New Regime Calculation: Gross income ₹8,00,000. Less standard deduction ₹75,000 = ₹7,25,000 taxable. No 80C, 80D, HRA, or NPS deductions allowed. Tax per new regime slabs: 5% on ₹3,25,000 (amount between ₹4L and ₹7.25L after nil slab) ≈ ₹16,250. Section 87A rebate may reduce this significantly depending on final taxable income. Estimated total tax: approximately ₹8,000–16,000.

For Rahul with moderate rent and active investments, the old regime saves roughly ₹5,000–10,000. But if Rahul had no rent and minimal investments, the new regime's higher standard deduction and rebate would win. The breakeven point depends on your specific combination of deductions — always calculate both before choosing.

Real Scenario: Ankit Paid ₹18,000 Extra Tax on ₹6 LPA

Ankit, a 26-year-old software developer in Hyderabad, earned ₹6,00,000 CTC. He chose the new tax regime by default when joining, assuming simpler is always better. His colleague Meera with identical salary chose the old regime. Ankit paid rent of ₹10,000/month (₹1,20,000/year), had EPF of ₹21,600, bought health insurance for ₹12,000/year, and invested ₹30,000 in ELSS.

Meera claimed HRA exemption of approximately ₹96,000 (minimum of HRA received, 50% of basic, and rent minus 10% of basic). She also claimed 80C for EPF plus ELSS (₹51,600), 80D for insurance (₹12,000), and paid zero additional tax after standard deduction of ₹50,000. Her taxable income dropped to approximately ₹3,90,400, keeping her in a low tax bracket with minimal liability.

Ankit, on the new regime, got only the ₹75,000 standard deduction with no HRA or 80C benefit. His taxable income was ₹5,25,000. After rebates, he still paid approximately ₹18,000 in total tax for the year — while Meera paid close to zero. Ankit's mistake was not calculating both regimes before submitting his tax declaration in April. When he switched to the old regime the following year and submitted proper rent receipts and investment proofs, he recovered the lost savings. The lesson: at ₹6 lakh with rent and investments, the old regime almost always wins.

Four Common Tax-Saving Mistakes

Mistake 1: Buying low-return insurance policies for 80C. Traditional life insurance policies offer 5–6% returns with long lock-ins. ELSS mutual funds provide market-linked returns with only a three-year lock-in and the same 80C benefit. Avoid mixing insurance and investment — buy term insurance for protection and ELSS or PPF for 80C savings.

Mistake 2: Missing the employer NPS window. Many employers offer NPS contribution through payroll with matching contributions under Section 80CCD(2). This employer contribution (up to 10% of basic) is deductible for the employer and not taxable as a perquisite for you up to specified limits. Missing this window means leaving free money on the table.

Mistake 3: Not submitting proofs on time. Declaring investments in April but failing to submit ELSS statements, rent receipts, or insurance premium receipts by the employer's deadline (usually January) causes your employer to deduct TDS without those exemptions. You can claim refunds through ITR, but your monthly cash flow suffers from excess TDS for most of the year.

Mistake 4: Ignoring the new regime rebate at ₹12 lakh. If you earn up to ₹12 lakh gross with minimal deductions, the new regime's Section 87A rebate can reduce your tax to zero. Switching to the old regime in this case would require you to actually spend money on investments and insurance to match the new regime's benefit. Always calculate both — the new regime is genuinely zero-tax for many mid-income earners in 2026.

Additional Deductions Worth Knowing

Beyond the big four (80C, 80D, 80CCD(1B), and HRA), salaried employees should be aware of: Section 24(b) — home loan interest deduction up to ₹2,00,000 for self-occupied property. Section 80E — education loan interest with no upper limit. Section 80G — donations to specified charitable organisations (50% or 100% deductible). Section 80TTA/80TTB — savings account interest deduction up to ₹10,000 (₹50,000 for senior citizens). Standard Deduction — ₹50,000 under old regime, ₹75,000 under new regime, applied automatically with no documentation required.

Frequently Asked Questions

Should I choose the old or new tax regime in 2026?

Choose the new regime if you have minimal deductions — no home loan interest, low rent, and limited 80C investments. It offers a higher standard deduction of ₹75,000 and a Section 87A rebate that can zero out tax for incomes up to ₹12 lakh. Choose the old regime if you pay significant rent (HRA exemption), have a home loan (Section 24b), max out 80C and 80D, and contribute to NPS. For most salaried employees with ₹8 lakh or more in gross income and active tax planning, the old regime still saves more tax.

What investments qualify under Section 80C?

Section 80C covers up to ₹1.5 lakh in specified investments and expenses: Employee Provident Fund (employee contribution), Public Provident Fund (PPF), ELSS mutual funds, National Savings Certificate (NSC), tax-saving fixed deposits (5-year lock-in), life insurance premiums, Sukanya Samriddhi Yojana, home loan principal repayment, and tuition fees for up to two children. EPF contributions are automatic for most salaried employees, so factor that in before planning additional investments to reach the ₹1.5 lakh cap.

How does HRA exemption reduce my taxable income?

HRA exemption under the old regime is the minimum of three amounts: actual HRA received, 50% of basic salary for metro cities (40% for non-metro), or actual rent paid minus 10% of basic salary. You must pay rent and submit valid rent receipts and landlord PAN (if annual rent exceeds ₹1 lakh). The exempt portion is subtracted from your taxable income. HRA exemption is not available under the new tax regime, which is a major reason high-rent payers in metro cities prefer the old regime.

What is the additional NPS deduction under Section 80CCD(1B)?

Section 80CCD(1B) allows an additional deduction of up to ₹50,000 for contributions to the National Pension System (NPS), over and above the ₹1.5 lakh limit of Section 80C. This is available under the old tax regime only. You can contribute through employer payroll (Section 80CCD(2) offers separate employer contribution benefits) or directly via the NPS portal. For someone in the 30% tax bracket, the full ₹50,000 NPS contribution saves approximately ₹15,600 in tax including cess.

Can I switch between old and new tax regime every year?

Salaried employees can switch between the old and new tax regime each financial year by informing their employer at the start of the year or when joining a new company. Once you file your ITR, the regime chosen in the return applies. Business income earners who opt out of the new regime cannot switch back easily. For salaried individuals, the flexibility means you should recalculate both regimes every April based on your expected rent, home loan, insurance premiums, and investment plans for the coming year.
