Home Loan EMI Complete Guide for First-Time Buyers India 2026

Understand EMI calculation, tenure trade-offs, prepayment savings, and affordability rules before you sign a 20-year home loan.

Published: 8 June 2026

Buying your first home in India is one of the largest financial decisions you will make. For most buyers, that purchase is financed through a home loan repaid via Equated Monthly Instalments (EMI) over 15 to 30 years. The EMI number on the sanction letter looks manageable — until you add registration charges, interior costs, maintenance, and the reality that interest can exceed the principal over long tenures.

This guide explains how EMI is calculated, compares tenures for a typical ₹30 lakh loan, shows how a ₹1 lakh prepayment in year five can save roughly ₹2.5 lakh in interest, covers the 40–50% salary rule banks use, walks through a real ₹35,000 salary scenario for a ₹25 lakh flat, and lists mistakes first-time buyers make. How Home Loan EMI Is Calculated

Indian home loans use the reducing balance method. Interest is charged each month only on the outstanding principal, not the original loan amount. As you pay down principal, interest component of EMI falls and principal component rises — though the total EMI stays fixed (for a standard fixed-rate loan).

The formula banks use is:

EMI = P × r × (1 + r)n / [(1 + r)n − 1]

Where:

  • P = Loan principal (amount borrowed)
  • r = Monthly interest rate = Annual rate ÷ 12 ÷ 100 (e.g. 8.5% → 0.007083)
  • n = Loan tenure in months (20 years = 240 months)

Example: ₹30,00,000 loan at 8.5% for 20 years gives EMI ≈ ₹26,035. In month one, interest on ₹30 lakh is about ₹21,250; only ₹4,785 goes to principal. By year 15, the split reverses — most of each EMI reduces principal. This front-loaded interest is why prepaying early saves disproportionately more.

₹30 Lakh Loan at 8.5%: Tenure Comparison

The biggest lever you control at sanction (besides loan amount) is tenure. Shorter tenure means higher EMI but dramatically less interest. Here is how ₹30 lakh at 8.5% annual interest compares across 10, 20, and 30 years:

Tenure Monthly EMI Total Payment Total Interest
10 years ₹37,196 ₹44,63,520 ₹14,63,520
20 years ₹26,035 ₹62,48,400 ₹32,48,400
30 years ₹23,067 ₹83,04,120 ₹53,04,120

The 30-year option saves ₹14,129 per month versus 10 years — but you pay ₹38.4 lakh more in total interest. That is more than the original loan. Many first-time buyers pick the longest tenure the bank offers because the EMI fits the spreadsheet. The hidden cost is wealth you never build because interest consumed it. If you must stretch tenure for affordability, plan prepayments from year two onward — even ₹5,000 extra per month makes a measurable difference.

Prepayment: ₹1 Lakh in Year 5 Saves ~₹2.5 Lakh Interest

Prepayment means paying extra toward principal before schedule. Most floating-rate home loans in India allow partial prepayment without penalty (RBI guidelines restrict prepayment charges on floating-rate loans from banks). Fixed-rate loans may carry fees — check your loan agreement.

Consider a ₹30 lakh loan at 8.5% for 20 years (EMI ₹26,035). After five years of regular payment, outstanding principal is roughly ₹26.5 lakh. If you prepay ₹1 lakh and choose to reduce tenure (keeping EMI same), you typically shave 8–10 months off the loan and save approximately ₹2.4–2.6 lakh in total interest — close to the ₹2.5 lakh rule of thumb cited by planners.

If you choose to reduce EMI instead, monthly cash flow improves but interest savings are lower because the outstanding principal accrues interest longer. For wealth maximisation, tenure reduction almost always wins unless you desperately need lower EMI for cash-flow reasons.

Best sources for prepayment: annual bonus, tax refund, maturity proceeds from FDs, or salary increments. Avoid prepaying home loan by stopping your emergency fund or equity SIP entirely — balance both. If your home loan rate is 8.5% and you can earn 12% long-term in equity, some planners argue investing surplus may beat prepayment; that is a risk trade-off, not a certainty. Guaranteed "return" from prepayment equals your loan interest rate.

The 40–50% Salary Rule for EMI Affordability

Banks assess home loan eligibility using FOIR (Fixed Obligation to Income Ratio). Total EMIs — including existing car, personal, and credit card EMIs — should not exceed 40–50% of net monthly income. Different banks use slightly different definitions of "net" (some deduct only statutory deductions; others are stricter).

Financial planners often recommend the lower end — 40% of take-home pay — for the home loan EMI alone, not all obligations combined. Why? Because homeownership costs extend beyond EMI: society maintenance, property tax, insurance, repairs, and utility spikes. If EMI consumes half your salary, one job loss or medical emergency can trigger default.

Quick reference for ₹35,000 in-hand salary:

  • 40% = ₹14,000 maximum recommended home EMI
  • 50% = ₹17,500 bank upper limit (risky as sole earner)

Co-applicant income (spouse, parent) increases eligible loan amount but does not reduce life risk if one income stops. Stress-test your budget at EMI + 2% rate hike (banks offer floating rates linked to repo; EMIs can rise when rates increase).

Real Scenario: ₹35,000 Salary, ₹25 Lakh Flat

Rahul, 29, works in Bengaluru with ₹35,000 in-hand salary after PF and TDS. He targets a ₹25 lakh ready-to-move flat. Banks typically finance up to 75–80% of property value, so he needs ₹5–6.25 lakh down payment plus registration (~7% in Karnataka) and basic furnishing — roughly ₹8–9 lakh total upfront cash.

Assuming ₹5 lakh down payment, loan amount = ₹20 lakh at 8.5%:

  • 20-year tenure — EMI ≈ ₹17,356. That is 49.6% of ₹35,000 in-hand — at the bank's ceiling and above the 40% comfort zone.
  • 25-year tenure — EMI ≈ ₹16,105. Still ~46% of income — tight for a single earner with no co-applicant.

Realistic paths for Rahul:

  1. Increase down payment to ₹10 lakh — ₹15 lakh loan at 20 years → EMI ≈ ₹13,017 (37% of salary). Comfortable range.
  2. Add spouse as co-applicant — Combined ₹60,000 in-hand; ₹17,356 EMI is 29%. Manageable with dual income buffer.
  3. Wait 18 months, save more — Delay purchase until down payment reduces loan below ₹18 lakh.
  4. Choose a ₹22 lakh property — Lower ticket size matches income today without over-leveraging.

Home loan interest up to ₹2 lakh and principal up to ₹1.5 lakh (within overall 80C cap) offer deductions in the old tax regime; the new regime does not allow these, which affects net cost of ownership.

Fixed vs Floating Rate and Other Costs

Most Indian home loans are floating-rate, linked to repo rate or bank MCLR/Benchmark. When RBI raises rates, your EMI or tenure adjusts. Fixed-rate loans offer certainty but often start 0.5–1% higher and may convert to floating after a fixed period. First-time buyers during stable or falling rate cycles often prefer floating; during sharp hikes, fixed portions via hybrid products appeal to risk-averse borrowers.

EMI is not your only cost. Budget for:

  • Processing fee (0.25–1% of loan, often negotiable)
  • Legal and technical verification charges
  • Insurance (home structure, term plan — many banks bundle; compare standalone policies)
  • Stamp duty and registration (state-specific, 5–7% in many metros)
  • GST on under-construction properties (no GST on ready-to-move with OC in most cases)

Four Mistakes First-Time Home Loan Buyers Make

  1. Maxing out tenure for the lowest EMI — A ₹3,000/month saving by choosing 30 years over 20 years can cost ₹20+ lakh in extra interest on a ₹30 lakh loan. Pick the shortest tenure you can sustain; prepay when possible.
  2. Ignoring total cost of ownership — ₹25 lakh flat becomes ₹33 lakh+ after interest, registration, and basic interiors. Compare "all-in" cost, not just property price tag.
  3. Skipping emergency fund before buying — Six months of expenses plus one EMI buffer should exist before you lock into a 20-year obligation. Without it, one missed EMI damages credit score and triggers penalty interest.
  4. Not reading the fine print on prepayment and rate reset — Some lenders cap free prepayments per year. Rate reset clauses determine how quickly your EMI drops when repo falls. Ask explicitly; do not assume.

Balancing Home Loan with Investments

A home loan is a liability; equity SIP is an asset builder. Many buyers stop all investments to afford EMI — understandable, but costly over decades. Even ₹2,000/month SIP alongside EMI preserves wealth-building habit. Our SIP investment guide explains how small monthly investments compound while you pay down the loan. Once prepayments bring outstanding principal below ₹10 lakh, some investors redirect surplus to SIP rather than further prepayment — a personal choice depending on risk tolerance and loan rate.

Frequently Asked Questions

How is home loan EMI calculated in India?

EMI is calculated using the reducing balance formula: EMI = P × r × (1+r)^n / ((1+r)^n − 1), where P is loan amount, r is monthly interest rate (annual rate ÷ 12 ÷ 100), and n is tenure in months. Each EMI pays interest on the outstanding balance first; the remainder reduces principal. Banks publish amortisation schedules showing month-by-month split. Online EMI calculators apply the same formula — verify bank quotes against an independent calculator before signing.

What percentage of salary should go toward home loan EMI?

Banks typically cap total EMIs at 40–50% of net monthly income across all loans. Financial planners often recommend keeping home loan EMI at or below 40% of take-home pay so you retain room for savings, insurance, emergencies, and lifestyle costs. Co-applicant income improves eligibility but should not stretch the household budget if one income is uncertain. Always stress-test at +2% interest rate to see if EMI remains affordable.

Is a longer home loan tenure better?

Longer tenure lowers monthly EMI but increases total interest paid substantially. A ₹30 lakh loan at 8.5% costs about ₹14.6 lakh interest over 10 years versus ₹53 lakh over 30 years — a difference of ₹38.4 lakh. Choose the shortest tenure you can comfortably afford. If you need a longer tenure initially, use annual prepayments from bonus or increments to shorten effective tenure without straining monthly cash flow.

Does prepayment reduce EMI or tenure?

Most banks let you choose. Reducing tenure keeps EMI the same but saves more interest overall — usually the better wealth-preserving option. Reducing EMI lowers monthly outflow but leaves principal outstanding longer, so total interest paid stays higher. Partial prepayment of ₹1 lakh in year 5 on a typical ₹30 lakh, 20-year loan at 8.5% can save roughly ₹2.5 lakh in interest if you reduce tenure rather than EMI.

Can I get a home loan with ₹35,000 monthly salary?

Yes, but approved loan amount depends on in-hand salary, existing EMIs, credit score, age, and property value. At ₹35,000 in-hand with no other loans, banks may approve home EMI around ₹14,000–₹17,500. That supports roughly ₹15–₹20 lakh loan over 20–25 years at 8.5%. A ₹25 lakh property typically requires ₹5–8 lakh down payment plus co-applicant income or a larger down payment to keep EMI within safe limits for a single earner.
