Complete Guide to Understanding Your Indian Salary — CTC vs In-Hand 2026

Everything Indian employees need to know about CTC, deductions, and take-home pay in FY 2026-27

Advertisement

When you receive your first offer letter from an Indian company, the number at the top — usually labelled CTC or Cost to Company — often feels like a promise of what you will earn. Then your first payslip arrives, and the amount credited to your bank account is noticeably smaller. This gap between CTC and in-hand salary confuses millions of first-time employees every year, and even experienced professionals sometimes misread their payslips when switching jobs or tax regimes. Understanding how your salary is structured, what gets deducted, and why those deductions exist is the foundation of smart financial planning in India.

This guide walks you through every component of a typical Indian salary package in 2026. You will learn what CTC actually includes, how gross salary differs from take-home pay, and the exact deductions that shrink your monthly credit — from Provident Fund and professional tax to TDS and health insurance premiums. We include a fully worked example for a ₹6 lakh per annum package, a real-world scenario for a 28-year-old starting their first job, and the four most common mistakes employees make when evaluating offers. By the end, you will be able to read any payslip with confidence and estimate your in-hand salary before accepting an offer.

What Is CTC (Cost to Company)?

CTC, or Cost to Company, represents the total annual expense an employer bears for employing you. It is not the amount you receive in your bank account. Think of it as the employer's all-in budget for your compensation package. CTC includes your gross salary (basic, HRA, special allowances, and bonuses), the employer's contribution to Provident Fund (typically 12% of basic, subject to the same ₹15,000 cap), gratuity accrual, group health insurance premiums, meal coupons, transport allowances, and any other benefits listed in your offer letter.

For example, if your offer states ₹6,00,000 CTC, that figure might break down as ₹5,40,000 in direct salary components and ₹60,000 in employer-side contributions and benefits. The ₹5,40,000 is your gross annual salary before any employee deductions. Many job seekers compare offers purely on CTC without examining the break-up, which can lead to disappointing take-home pay even when the headline number looks attractive. Two offers with identical CTC but different basic-to-allowance ratios can produce in-hand salaries that differ by ₹3,000 to ₹5,000 per month.

CTC vs Gross Salary vs In-Hand Salary

These three terms are often used interchangeably, but they mean different things. Gross salary is your total earnings before any deductions — it includes basic pay, HRA, conveyance allowance, special allowance, and any fixed monthly bonus. Gross salary is always less than or equal to CTC because CTC adds employer-side costs on top. In-hand salary, also called net salary or take-home pay, is what remains after all employee-side deductions are subtracted from your gross salary.

The typical deduction chain works like this: Gross Salary minus Employee PF minus Professional Tax minus TDS minus Health Insurance (employee share) minus any other voluntary deductions equals In-Hand Salary. Employer PF contributions, gratuity accruals, and insurance premiums paid entirely by the company are part of CTC but never appear as deductions on your payslip because they are employer costs, not employee deductions. Keeping this distinction clear prevents the most common budgeting mistake — planning your rent and EMIs based on CTC divided by twelve.

Understanding Salary Components

Most Indian companies structure salaries using a few standard components. Basic salary typically accounts for 40% to 50% of CTC and forms the base for PF, gratuity, and HRA calculations. House Rent Allowance (HRA) is usually 40% of basic for non-metro cities and 50% for metro cities like Mumbai, Delhi, Bangalore, and Chennai. HRA can be partially or fully tax-exempt under the old tax regime if you pay rent and submit valid receipts. Special allowance is a flexible component that fills the gap between basic plus HRA and your target gross salary — it is fully taxable under both regimes.

Other common components include conveyance or transport allowance (up to ₹1,600 per month was exempt under old rules, though many companies now fold this into special allowance), leave travel allowance (LTA), food coupons or meal vouchers, and performance-linked variable pay. Bonuses and joining bonuses are part of CTC but may be paid separately and taxed in the month received. When evaluating an offer, ask for a detailed break-up showing each component as a monthly and annual figure. This single step can save you from unpleasant surprises on your first payday.

All Deductions Explained

Provident Fund (PF / EPF) — 12% Capped at ₹1,800

The Employee Provident Fund is a mandatory retirement savings scheme for organisations with 20 or more employees. Both you and your employer contribute 12% of your basic salary each month. However, the EPFO caps the contributory basic at ₹15,000 per month. This means your maximum employee PF deduction is 12% × ₹15,000 = ₹1,800 per month, even if your actual basic is ₹25,000 or higher. Your employer matches this with another ₹1,800 (plus administrative charges). The combined corpus earns tax-free interest and becomes accessible at retirement or after two months of unemployment. Some employees voluntarily contribute more through VPF, which increases deductions but builds a larger retirement corpus.

Professional Tax — Varies by State

Professional tax is a state government levy on salaried individuals. It is deducted monthly by your employer and remitted to the state. The amount varies significantly: Maharashtra and Karnataka typically deduct ₹200 per month for most salaried employees earning above the threshold. West Bengal charges up to ₹250 per month. Tamil Nadu has a slab-based system ranging from zero to ₹2,500 per year. Delhi, Haryana, and Rajasthan do not levy professional tax at all. If you work remotely from a different state than your employer's registered office, the deduction usually follows your office location, not your home address. Professional tax is deductible from taxable income under both tax regimes.

Tax Deducted at Source (TDS)

TDS is income tax withheld by your employer each month based on your projected annual income. The amount depends on whether you choose the old or new tax regime, your declared investments under Section 80C, HRA exemption claims, and other deductions. Under the new tax regime for FY 2026-27, a standard deduction of ₹75,000 applies, and a Section 87A rebate can reduce tax to zero for taxable incomes up to ₹12 lakh. Under the old regime, the standard deduction is ₹50,000, but you can claim HRA, 80C (up to ₹1.5 lakh), 80D medical insurance, and other exemptions. Your employer estimates annual tax liability in April and spreads deductions across twelve months. Submitting investment proofs late can cause a sudden spike in TDS during January through March.

Health Insurance Premiums

Many employers provide group health insurance covering you and sometimes your family. The premium cost appears in CTC, but the deduction experience varies. If your employer pays the entire premium, you see no deduction — it is purely an employer cost. If you opt for top-up coverage or family floater add-ons, the employee share (typically ₹500 to ₹2,000 per month) is deducted from your salary. Group health insurance premiums paid by the employer are not taxable as a perquisite up to certain limits. Premiums you pay separately for personal health insurance qualify for deduction under Section 80D — up to ₹25,000 for self and family, and an additional ₹50,000 for senior citizen parents.

Other Common Deductions

Depending on your company, you may also see deductions for employee stock purchase plans, meal coupon contributions (if not fully employer-funded), transport deductions, loan repayments (salary advances or company loans), and voluntary NPS contributions under Section 80CCD(1). Each of these reduces your in-hand salary but may offer tax benefits or long-term value. Review your payslip line by line in the first month and clarify anything unfamiliar with HR.

Worked Example: ₹6 LPA with 40% Basic

Let us calculate the in-hand salary for a ₹6,00,000 CTC package with 40% basic, typical of many entry-level IT and corporate roles in 2026. This example assumes the new tax regime, employment in Karnataka, and standard employer health insurance with no employee premium share.

Annual salary break-up: Basic = ₹2,40,000 (₹20,000/month). HRA = ₹96,000 (40% of basic, ₹8,000/month). Special Allowance = ₹2,64,000 (₹22,000/month). Gross annual salary = ₹6,00,000 (₹50,000/month). Note that employer PF contribution of approximately ₹21,600 per year is part of CTC but not part of gross salary in most structures.

Monthly deductions: Employee PF = ₹1,800 (12% of capped basic ₹15,000). Professional Tax = ₹200. TDS = approximately ₹0 to ₹520 per month under the new regime after the ₹75,000 standard deduction and applicable rebates on ₹5,25,000 taxable income. Total deductions ≈ ₹2,000–2,520 per month.

Estimated in-hand salary: ₹47,480 to ₹48,000 per month, or roughly ₹5,69,760 to ₹5,76,000 annually. This represents about 79–80% of monthly gross. Use our salary calculator to model your exact break-up with different basic percentages and tax regimes.

Real Scenario: Priya, 28, First Job in Bangalore

Priya graduated with an MBA and joined a mid-size tech company in Bangalore at ₹6,00,000 CTC. Her offer letter showed an impressive number, and she calculated she could afford ₹15,000 rent plus ₹8,000 in expenses — expecting ₹50,000 monthly. On her first payday, ₹47,650 hit her account. Confused, she checked her payslip: Basic ₹20,000, HRA ₹8,000, Special Allowance ₹22,000, Gross ₹50,000. Deductions: PF ₹1,800, Professional Tax ₹200, TDS ₹350, and ₹0 for health insurance (employer-paid). Total deductions: ₹2,350.

Priya's mistake was dividing CTC by twelve. She also did not realise that HRA exemption requires rent receipts under the old regime — she had chosen the new regime by default, so her ₹8,000 HRA was taxable in principle but offset by the higher standard deduction and rebate. After reading her offer break-up carefully, Priya adjusted her budget to ₹47,000 in-hand, started a ₹5,000 SIP with the difference from her original plan, and submitted Form 12BB to ensure TDS was spread evenly. Six months later, when she received a ₹8,00,000 offer from a competitor, she compared in-hand pay using a calculator rather than CTC — and made a smarter career decision.

Four Common Mistakes When Reading Your Salary

Mistake 1: Comparing offers on CTC alone. Two ₹8 lakh packages can produce in-hand salaries differing by ₹4,000 per month depending on basic percentage, PF structure, and tax regime. Always compare gross and estimated net pay side by side.

Mistake 2: Ignoring the tax regime choice. The new regime offers simplicity and higher standard deduction but no HRA or 80C benefits. The old regime rewards those with home loans, rent payments, and disciplined tax-saving investments. Choosing wrong can cost ₹15,000 to ₹50,000 in annual tax. Read our tax saving guide for a detailed comparison.

Mistake 3: Forgetting about joining bonus taxation. A ₹1 lakh joining bonus is attractive, but it is taxed at your marginal rate in the month received. Your TDS that month will spike, and your in-hand salary for that month may look unusually low in subsequent months when annual TDS is recalibrated.

Mistake 4: Not accounting for variable pay. Many offers include 10–20% variable pay tied to performance. If your CTC is ₹10 lakh with 15% variable, your guaranteed fixed CTC is only ₹8.5 lakh. Budget based on fixed components, and treat variable pay as a bonus rather than guaranteed income.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between CTC and in-hand salary in India?

CTC (Cost to Company) is the total annual amount your employer spends on you, including your gross pay, employer PF contribution, gratuity accrual, insurance premiums, and other benefits. In-hand salary is what actually lands in your bank account each month after employee-side deductions such as PF, professional tax, TDS, and any voluntary contributions. A ₹6 lakh CTC does not mean ₹50,000 in-hand — after typical deductions, expect roughly ₹44,000–47,000 depending on your salary structure and tax regime.

Why is my PF deduction capped at ₹1,800 per month?

Employee Provident Fund contributions are calculated as 12% of your basic salary, but the EPFO rules cap the contributory basic at ₹15,000 per month. That means the maximum employee PF deduction is 12% of ₹15,000, which equals ₹1,800 per month, regardless of whether your actual basic is ₹20,000 or ₹40,000. Your employer also contributes a matching amount. Amounts above this cap can go into a Voluntary Provident Fund (VPF) account if you opt in, but that is a separate voluntary deduction.

How much professional tax is deducted from salary?

Professional tax is a state-level levy, so the amount varies. In Maharashtra and Karnataka, most salaried employees pay ₹200 per month once their salary crosses the threshold. West Bengal charges up to ₹250 per month. Some states like Delhi and Haryana do not levy professional tax at all. Your employer deducts this automatically and remits it to the state government. It is a small deduction, but it is mandatory where applicable and appears on every payslip.

Can I negotiate a higher in-hand salary with the same CTC?

Yes, salary structure directly affects your take-home pay even when CTC stays constant. A higher basic salary increases PF deductions and may push you into a higher tax bracket, while a structure heavy on HRA and special allowances can reduce taxable income under the old tax regime. Under the new regime, HRA is not exempt, so a lower basic with more allowances may actually increase in-hand pay. Discuss the break-up with HR before accepting an offer — a ₹50,000 CTC structured differently can change monthly in-hand by ₹2,000–4,000.

When does TDS get deducted from my salary?

Tax Deducted at Source (TDS) is withheld by your employer every month based on your projected annual income, chosen tax regime, and declared investments or exemptions. At the start of the financial year, you submit Form 12BB with rent receipts, 80C proofs, and other declarations. Your employer calculates estimated annual tax, divides it by 12, and deducts that amount monthly. If you switch jobs mid-year or fail to submit proofs, you may face a large TDS adjustment in the final months or a tax refund or demand when filing your ITR.
